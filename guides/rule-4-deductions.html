Rule 4 Deductions Explained

Rule 4 applies when a horse is withdrawn from a race after you've placed your bet. Here's how it works and how to calculate your adjusted return.

What is a Rule 4 Deduction?

A Rule 4 deduction is a reduction applied to your winnings when a horse is withdrawn from a race (becomes a non-runner) after you placed your bet. Because the non-runner would have taken money away from the other horses winning, bookmakers reduce your winnings to compensate.

The deduction is based on the starting price (SP) or odds of the non-runner at the time of withdrawal. The shorter the withdrawn horse's odds, the larger the deduction (because a short-priced horse was more likely to win, so other horses benefit more from its removal).

Important: Rule 4 deductions apply to winnings only — your original stake is not affected.

Rule 4 Calculation Example

You bet £10 on Horse A to win @ 5/1 (6.0 decimal)
Horse B (a 2/1 favourite) is withdrawn = 15p Rule 4

Without Rule 4:
  Winnings = £10 × (6.0 − 1) = £50
  Total return = £60

With Rule 4 (15p deduction on winnings):
  Adjusted winnings = £50 × (1 − 0.15) = £50 × 0.85 = £42.50
  Total return = £42.50 + £10 (stake) = £52.50

FAQ

Yes. Rule 4 deductions apply to both the win part and the place part of an each-way bet.

Yes. If more than one horse is withdrawn, a Rule 4 deduction applies for each non-runner. The total maximum deduction is 75p in the £1. Multiple deductions are applied cumulatively.

If you took a fixed price before the race, Rule 4 applies based on the withdrawn horse's SP or price at time of withdrawal. If you took SP (starting price), the SP already reflects the non-runner so no further deduction usually applies.
